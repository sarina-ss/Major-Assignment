The Twilight Zone
 

The twilight zone is just below the sunlight zone and stretches up to 1000m in depth. Due to how deep it is in this zone, water pressure can reach up to 1,500 pounds per square inch! There is a variety of organisms who have adapted to these conditions living in the twilight zone. This includes:

  • Microscopic bacteria
  • Zooplankton
  • Crustaceans
  • Fish
  • Squid
  • Gelatinous animals

 
 
Animals in the twilight zone use a mechanism similar to countershading, called counterillumination. Since there is some sunlight seeping into the twilight zone, predators have a tendency to look upwards to find their prey. However, some animals that are targetted as prey have photophores along their bellies. These photophores can emit light that disguises them against the light of the surface water, making them invisible to the predators looking for them from below. Although photophores play a role in the adaptation of some animals in the dark zone, counterillumination is more prominent in the twilight zone since some light can still reach this depth.
